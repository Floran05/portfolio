  • Drone simulator

    As you'll read below in the sesion describing my hobbies, I'm a FPV drone pilot. This discipline has on drawback: a small piloting error can lead to a drone crash, and potentially high repair costs. One way of avoiding such errors is to train on a simulator, but simulators also often have a drawback, which is the fidelity of physics to reality, and more specifically the effect of gravity. In most simulators, when you let the drone fall (it's common to have to put the throttle to minimum), it loses altitude much more slowly than in real-life conditions.

    This problem made me want to test for myself and create a prototype drone flight model on Unreal Engine, to understant how the physics engine works and, by extension, why most simulators suffer from this inaccuracy compared to reality.

    The answer (at least in the case of Unreal Engine), is a coefficient used to simulate air friction during the fall. This method provides an approximation, but the result is still a long way from reality, at least in the context of drone piloting, which requires enormous precision.

  • AI-based skills identification system

    During my last year of master's degree, I had the chance to carry out an internship in the FEMTO-ST laboratory, with a project in the field of artificial intelligence, an area that seemed so complex that i tought it inaccessible.

    The objective of this project was to carry out an application module capable of identifying the skills that a student masters, by analizing an evaluation exercise. This module was intended to be integrated into a course revisions application, on which I was able to work, and which I detail in the next tile.

    So I discovered the Natural Language Processing (NLP) AI techniques, with in particular the multilabel classification technique, which I used in this project. This technique consists in teaching artificial intelligence how to associate elements of a text with a label, so that it is later, capable of carrying out the reverse : identifying all labels in a new text.

    In our case, the labels represented the skills to be identified and we were able to train AI for algorithmic and english. Thus, the skills could be on the one hand, "knowing how to write a conditional structure", and on the other, "knowing how to use -ing verbs".

    This AI can therefore, in theory, apply to any subject, taking into account that it still takes a minimum amount of data to train it. This notion posed us problem during the project, because it is more difficult to obtain correct results with an AI trained on a very poor dataset.

  • Course revision application

    Following the final year of my Master's project, the FEMTO-ST laboratory offered me the chance to continue the project for a further two years. I took part in the development of the Artificial Intelligence Virtual Trainer (AI-VT) application, which already existed when I arrived. Based on a case-based reasoning system, it enables students to revise their courses by generating a personalized list of execises for each revision session. In the version I worked on, personalization was enabled by the teacher, who had to mark each exercise completed by each student. In this way, the system was able to identify the student's progress and propose a progression in the exercises.

    My first task was to integrate the module created during the internship into the application. The aim of this module was to create a profile of a student using the application for the first time, identifying the skills he still needs to work on, so that he could have a personalized experience right from the first revision exercises.

    I subsequently redeveloped and modernized the application, originally coded in PHP in 2014, rewriting it in Javascript with NodeJS and improving the interfaces to make them more up-to-date.

    A new player was later added to the project : a humanoid robot, Nao. The aim was to make revision sessions more dynamic by adding interaction between the student and the robot. I was in charge of interfacing the application with Nao to enable ergonomic control of the robot, but also to enable it to react automatically during a revision session.

    Whereas the classic revision system could be used by any type of user, the sessions with Nao were aimed at a younger audience. We had the opportunity to carry out an experiment in a nursery school, to make pupils revise English lessons. For example, Nao could ask what color his eyes were, or show him a card with a certain number written on it (the robot identified the cards using a QR code).

  • Cholera data formatting application for the Democratic Republic of Congo

    For my 3rd year Bachelor's degree internship in the Chrono-environnement laboratory, the project concerned data on cholera in the Democratic Republic of Congo. The aim was to create an application that would enable scientists to format their data easily and in different formats, and to generate PDF reports of the conclusions they could draw from it.

    The application was built as a web application with a server side in PHP, and its primary aim was to offer its features in an ergonomic and simple way, so that it could be used by people who are not specialists in computer sciences. This meant that data could be imported and displayed in the form of tables, graphs or maps. Once the scientist had filtered the data he wished to highlight, he could add the table, graph or map to a report and add his comments, finally exporting the report as a PDF.

    Towards the end of the project, the application was deployed on a laboratory server for internal access.

Paragon: details of some implemented features

Deck builder

Whereas League of Legends uses a system of items store, which players can purchase as the game proggresses, Paragon is based on a card deck system. In order to adapt to different team compositions, players must first build several decks. During this process, you need to equip gems (left side of the interface) and cards (central part of the interface). Gems and cards have characteristics that influence the character's stats, or offer passives. Cards can also provide an active skill. They all have an affinity that defines their play style (e.g. Chaos (Wold) ⇒ damage, Growth (Flower) ⇒ defense), and a deck can only contain cards of two different affinities.

Demonstration

In-game shop

Once players have earned 1000 coins during the game, they can spend them on an attribute point (Agility, Vitality or Intellect). When the player has enough attribute points (the cost is indicated in the top right corner of each card), he can equip a card in one of the three solts.

Demonstration

Levels and abilities

Characters can gain experience and level up by performing various actions (killing an enemy player, destroying a turret, etc.). Each level up allows the player to unlock or improve a character's skill.

Demonstration

Turrets and minions

To win the game, players must destroy the opposing team's Core (a building in the player's spawn area), which is protected by turrets on each line of the map. Players must therefore advance along each line, destroying the turrets blocking the way without beingg killed by them. They must allow the minions to advance, so that they can take the turrets' damage instead of the player

Demonstration

Vision system

Players can move discreetly around the map to surprise their opponents. To do this, they need to stay out of direct line-of-sight with an opposing player. If an opponent is spotted, the whole team is informed of his position.

Demonstration

Quick communication system

A quick communication system allows players to transmit essential information to the whole team without having to write a message in the chat. The system also allows you to send a message that will be automatically translated into the language of the other players.
